Investing & Personal Finance Glossary

Plain-English definitions of the investing, halal-finance, and money terms every beginner meets, each linked to a full guide.

Asset Allocation

Asset allocation is how you divide your money across different types of investments (stocks, bonds, and cash) to balance risk and reward. For example, a young investor might keep 70% in stocks and 30% in safer bonds. Learn more →

Bear Market

A bear market is a prolonged period when prices fall by 20% or more from a recent high and investor mood turns pessimistic. The KSE-100 dropping sharply over many months would be a classic example. Learn more →

Book Value

Book value is what a company would be worth on paper if it sold everything it owns and paid off all its debts, calculated as total assets minus total liabilities. If a firm has Rs 100 of assets and Rs 40 of debt, its book value is Rs 60. Learn more →

Brokerage Account

A brokerage account is an account with a licensed broker that lets you buy and sell shares on a stock exchange like the PSX. In Pakistan you open one by registering with a TREC-holder broker and the CDC (Central Depository Company). Learn more →

Bull Market

A bull market is a sustained period when prices are rising and investors feel optimistic about the outlook. When the KSE-100 climbs steadily for months, the market is described as bullish. Learn more →

Candlestick

A candlestick is a chart symbol that shows a stock's open, close, high, and low price for a given time period in a single shape. A green (or hollow) candle means the price closed higher than it opened, while a red one means it closed lower. Learn more →

Capital Gain

A capital gain is the profit you make when you sell an investment for more than you paid for it. Buying a PSX share at Rs 100 and selling at Rs 130 produces a Rs 30 capital gain, which may be subject to capital gains tax. Learn more →

Compound Interest

Compound interest means earning returns not just on your original money but also on the returns it has already generated, so growth accelerates over time. Rs 100,000 growing at 10% a year becomes about Rs 110,000 after year one, then earns 10% on that larger amount the following year. Learn more →

Debt-to-Equity Ratio

A measure of how much a company relies on borrowed money versus its owners' money, found by dividing total debt by shareholders' equity. A high ratio signals more debt and more risk, and in halal screening, too much interest-based debt can make a stock non-compliant. Learn more →

Diversification

Spreading your money across many different investments so one bad performer doesn't sink your whole portfolio. Owning shares in several sectors instead of just one bank is a straightforward example of diversification. Learn more →

Dividend

A share of a company's profits paid out to shareholders, usually in cash. If you own 100 shares and the company pays a Rs 5 per-share dividend, you receive Rs 500. Learn more →

Dollar-Cost Averaging

Investing a fixed amount at regular intervals regardless of price, so you buy more shares when prices are low and fewer when they are high. Putting Rs 10,000 into an index fund every month is a classic example of dollar-cost averaging. Learn more →

Earnings Per Share (EPS)

A company's net profit divided by its total number of shares, showing how much profit each share earned. If a company makes Rs 1 billion in profit and has 100 million shares outstanding, its EPS is Rs 10. Learn more →

Emergency Fund

Easily accessible savings set aside to cover unexpected costs like medical bills or job loss, so you don't have to sell investments or borrow money in a pinch. A common goal is three to six months of living expenses. Learn more →

ETF (Exchange-Traded Fund)

A basket of many stocks or other assets bundled into one security you can buy and sell like a single share. A KMI-30 tracking ETF lets you own a slice of 30 Sharia-compliant companies in one trade. Learn more →

Fundamental Analysis

Studying a company's financial health (profits, debt, growth, and management) to judge whether its stock is a good long-term value. It answers the question of whether a business is worth owning, rather than just watching the share price move. Learn more →

Gharar

Gharar refers to excessive uncertainty, ambiguity, or speculation in a contract, which Islam prohibits in financial dealings. It is a key reason many scholars consider gambling-like trading, and some derivatives, to be non-halal. Learn more →

Index Fund

An index fund is a low-cost fund designed to match a market index rather than beat it, by holding the same stocks the index holds. An index fund tracking the KSE-100 rises and falls roughly in line with that index. Learn more →

Inflation

Inflation is the gradual rise in prices over time, which shrinks how much your money can buy. If inflation runs at 12% a year, Rs 100 buys noticeably less the following year, which is one reason people invest rather than holding only cash. Learn more →

IPO (Initial Public Offering)

An IPO is the first time a private company sells its shares to the public and lists on a stock exchange. After an IPO on the PSX, anyone with a brokerage account can buy that company's shares. Learn more →

KMI All-Share Index

The KMI All-Share Index is a PSX index that includes all Sharia-compliant companies listed on the exchange, screened using Meezan Bank's methodology. It lets Muslim investors track the broad halal market, much like the KSE-100 tracks the overall market. Learn more →

Limit Order

A limit order is an instruction to buy or sell a stock only at a specific price or better, rather than at whatever the current market price happens to be. For example, you might place a limit order to buy a share only if it drops to Rs 95 or below. Learn more →

Market Capitalization

Market capitalization is the total value of a company's shares, calculated by multiplying the share price by the number of shares outstanding. A company with 100 million shares priced at Rs 50 each has a market cap of Rs 5 billion. Learn more →

Market Order

A market order is an instruction to buy or sell a stock immediately at the best price currently available. It guarantees the trade happens quickly, but not the exact price you will receive. Learn more →

Moving Average

A line on a chart showing a stock's average price over a recent period, smoothing out daily noise to reveal the underlying trend. A 50-day moving average, for example, plots the average closing price across the last 50 trading days. Learn more →

Mutual Fund

A pool of money from many investors that a professional manager puts to work across a mix of stocks, bonds, or other assets. Pakistan also offers Islamic (Sharia-compliant) mutual funds that steer clear of prohibited sectors. Learn more →

Net Worth

Net worth is simply what you own minus what you owe, giving you the clearest snapshot of your overall financial health. If your assets total Rs 5 million and your debts are Rs 1 million, your net worth is Rs 4 million. Learn more →

P/E Ratio (Price-to-Earnings)

The share price divided by earnings per share, showing how much investors pay for each rupee of a company's profit. A P/E of 10 means you pay Rs 10 for every Rs 1 of annual earnings, which makes it a handy yardstick for comparing how cheap or expensive stocks are. Learn more →

Portfolio

The complete collection of investments you own, such as your stocks, funds, and bonds considered together as one picture. Reviewing your portfolio means stepping back to assess all your holdings at once rather than one at a time. Learn more →

Purification

In halal investing, purification means donating to charity the small slice of your returns that came from impermissible sources (such as a company's interest income) in order to cleanse the gain. Investors calculate the haram percentage and give it away, expecting no personal reward for doing so. Learn more →

Return on Equity (ROE)

A measure of how efficiently a company turns shareholders' money into profit, calculated by dividing net income by shareholders' equity. An ROE of 20% means the company earns Rs 20 of profit for every Rs 100 of owners' money invested. Learn more →

Riba

Riba refers to interest or usury, meaning any guaranteed and predetermined return on a loan, which is strictly forbidden in Islam. Earning fixed interest on a conventional bank deposit or bond is riba, which is why Muslims seek halal alternatives such as profit-sharing arrangements. Learn more →

Risk Tolerance

How much volatility in your investments you can handle, both financially and emotionally, without panicking. Someone who would lose sleep over a 20% drop has a low risk tolerance and should stick to safer assets. Learn more →

RSI (Relative Strength Index)

A technical indicator scaled from 0 to 100 that signals whether a stock may be overbought or oversold. Readings above 70 often suggest it is overbought (possibly due for a fall) and readings below 30 suggest it is oversold (possibly due for a bounce). Learn more →

Sharia Screening

The process of checking whether a stock meets Islamic rules, filtering out prohibited businesses (alcohol, gambling, conventional banking) and companies carrying too much interest-based debt or income. Index providers like KMI apply these filters to build halal stock lists. Learn more →

Short Selling

A strategy that bets on a stock falling by borrowing shares, selling them, and hoping to buy them back at a lower price later. Most scholars consider it non-halal because you are selling something you do not own. Learn more →

Stock (Share)

A unit of ownership in a company, entitling the holder to a slice of its profits and growth. Buying one Lucky Cement share means you own a tiny piece of that company. Learn more →

Stock Split

When a company divides its existing shares into more shares, lowering the price of each without changing the total value you hold. In a 2-for-1 split, your 100 shares at Rs 200 become 200 shares at Rs 100. Learn more →

Sukuk

Islamic investment certificates often compared to bonds, except that instead of paying interest they give holders a share of real assets or projects and the income those assets generate. They are structured to comply with Sharia by avoiding riba. Learn more →

Support and Resistance

Price levels on a chart where a stock has repeatedly stopped falling (support) or stopped rising (resistance). Traders watch these levels closely because prices often bounce or stall at them. Learn more →

Technical Analysis

Technical analysis means studying price charts and trading patterns to forecast where a stock might go next, rather than examining the underlying business. It focuses on questions like "what is the price doing?" using tools such as candlesticks and moving averages. Learn more →

Zakat

Zakat is the obligatory annual charity in Islam, typically 2.5% of qualifying wealth held for a full lunar year, including the market value of shares you hold for investment. A Muslim with Rs 1,000,000 of zakatable stock would generally owe Rs 25,000 in zakat. Learn more →
